About me

Why a business & law grad
gets it

My route into product thinking came through evidence, analysis and problem solving rather than design. Throughout my degree I applied academic research methods and established frameworks to analyse business processes, consumer behaviour, supply chains, organisational strategy and ethics. That kind of structured, critical thinking sits at the foundation of UX research, product management and business analysis.

The SITE Oldham project is a strong example of that in practice. I spent months investigating why people had stopped engaging with their local high street, examining the interplay between local businesses, public services, funding constraints and community wellbeing. I identified the key barriers, brought together the evidence and presented a structured set of recommendations to address them.

We earned a First Class grade and were recognised in the top three at the NIBS national competition.

Agent Academy represents an opportunity to step into a digital, creative environment where I can apply the analytical and research skills I have developed, contributing to work that shapes how people experience products, how organisations operate or how teams get things done.

What I'd look into next

Evaluating the first-time consumer experience from app launch through to product discovery.

01
Localisation asked upfront Good
The first screen asks the consumer to confirm their location, currency and language before anything else. It is an uncommon first step and signals that the app is setting itself up around the consumer's context rather than making assumptions.

Business rationale: Pricing, delivery options and product availability vary by region. Collecting this upfront means the consumer sees a more relevant experience from the start and is less likely to be caught out by unexpected costs further down the line.
02
No sign-up required to browse Good
Consumers land directly in a product feed without being asked to create an account. This keeps entry low-pressure and lets the catalogue build interest before any commitment is requested.

Worth noting: An email address or phone number is still required at checkout, so the requirement is delayed rather than removed entirely.
03
Notification request and coupon pop-up appear before any browsing
Almost immediately after opening the app, a push notification permission request and a coupon overlay appear. No products have been viewed at this point, so neither feels relevant to where the consumer actually is.

Business rationale: Capturing notification permissions early maximises the opportunity to re-engage consumers who leave without buying. Surfacing coupons at the start sets up a value-driven first impression before consumers have assessed prices independently.

What I would change: Delay both until after a meaningful interaction. A notification prompt after a consumer saves an item, or coupons introduced at the basket stage, would feel far more timely and relevant.

Evaluating the product discovery experience as a consumer searching for something specific.

01
Strong filtering and functional search Good
A wide range of filters and a well-functioning search make it possible to narrow the catalogue effectively. For a platform of this size, that is essential infrastructure and it largely delivers.
02
Inconsistent sizing information
Some products include detailed measurements; others only list generic size labels with no additional guidance. For consumers buying without being able to try items on, this inconsistency creates real uncertainty and is likely a contributing factor to high return rates.

Business rationale: Shein works with a very large number of independent suppliers, so consistent product information across all of them is an operational challenge rather than a deliberate design choice.

What I would change: Introduce minimum sizing requirements at the point of product submission and increase the visibility of consumer reviews that reference fit.
03
Coupon prompts reappear throughout browsing
Coupon overlays resurfaced multiple times during browsing, despite having already been dismissed at the onboarding stage. The repetition interrupts the browsing experience without adding anything new.

What I would change: One well-placed reminder is useful. Repeated prompts across the same session work against the browsing experience rather than supporting it.

Examining the checkout flow from cart through to the point of purchase.

01
Heavy use of urgency cues throughout the cart
The cart features a high volume of animated prompts: product images moving side to side, stock warnings such as "8 left" cycling every few seconds, prices highlighted in red, and repeated low stock alerts. The overall effect is a checkout environment that feels pressuring rather than helpful to a consumer trying to make a considered decision.

Business rationale: Urgency-based prompts are a well-known tactic for increasing the proportion of consumers who complete a purchase. At Shein's scale, even a small uplift across a large number of sessions carries real commercial value.

The wider concern: Consumers who feel rushed into purchases they were unsure about are more likely to return items and less likely to trust the platform over time.
02
Contact details required before proceeding to checkout
Completing a purchase requires providing an email address or phone number. By this point time has been spent selecting items, making it more likely a consumer will comply, but it is still a barrier that was not flagged earlier in the journey.

What I would change: A guest checkout option would reduce the friction here, with an optional account creation step introduced after the purchase is complete.
03
Delivery costs only visible late in the checkout process
Delivery fees are not shown in the cart. They only appear when selecting a delivery option, several steps into checkout. By that point the consumer has already invested time and effort, making it harder to step back when the total is higher than expected.

What I would change: Show an estimated delivery cost at the cart stage before checkout begins. PrettyLittleThing handles this well, displaying the delivery tier and cost upfront, which builds confidence in the process rather than catching the consumer off guard at the end.
04
Product recommendations placed next to the checkout button
Additional product suggestions appear at the bottom of the cart, close to the checkout button. At the point where a consumer has already decided what they want, introducing new options creates distraction rather than value.

What I would change: Move these to the order confirmation screen, where the consumer is in a more positive mindset and may genuinely be open to seeing more.

Each observation raised a question worth looking into further. These are the three I would prioritise.

01
Do consumers notice the urgency prompts in the cart, and does it affect how much they trust the brand over time?
The cart is built around creating a sense of urgency. If consumers start to realise that some of those prompts are not genuine, for example noticing that a stock warning resets, it raises a question about whether that affects their longer-term relationship with the brand even if it drives purchases in the short term.

How I would approach it: Sit with consumers as they move through the checkout and ask them to talk through what they are noticing and how it is making them feel. A short follow-up conversation would help surface whether they identified any of the urgency prompts and what it did to their overall impression of the brand.
02
Does revealing delivery costs late lead to more consumers dropping out before completing their order?
Delivery fees only appear deep in the checkout flow. I would want to know whether consumers drop off at that point because the total is higher than expected, and whether showing the cost earlier would result in more completed orders rather than fewer.

How I would approach it: Survey recent Shein customers about when they first noticed the delivery cost and how it affected their decision. Looking at where consumers tend to exit the checkout process would add useful context.
03
Do product recommendations next to checkout help sales or get in the way of completing a purchase?
Showing additional products just before checkout could encourage consumers to add more to their order, or it could distract them from completing the purchase they have already decided to make. It would also be worth knowing whether first-time and returning consumers respond to it differently.

How I would approach it: Test two versions of the cart, one with and one without the recommendations, and compare how many consumers complete checkout in each. Splitting the results by new and returning consumers would show whether the placement works differently for each group.

Consumer research
Young Enterprise: Foldable Water Bottle
Team project · First Class · National Top 3
Our team developed a foldable water bottle concept positioned around sustainability and modern consumer lifestyle trends. I led the market research, conducting primary research through surveys, polls and direct consumer feedback to analyse demand, purchasing motivations and target demographic preferences, as well as researching cultural attitudes towards sustainability to inform our positioning. We refined the concept continuously based on what we found. Progress was presented bi-weekly in structured updates, and the final concept was pitched to external industry assessors at a trade stand, similar in format to Dragons' Den, as well as to academic assessors for other elements of the project. We earned a First Class grade and were recognised in the top three at the NIBS national competition.
